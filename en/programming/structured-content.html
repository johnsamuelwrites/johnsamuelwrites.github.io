Structured Content

What is structured content? There may be multiple responses depending on the audience.

Human Readability

Let's start with a use case, where our target audience is human readers. In this case, the goal of structured content1 or structured data is to ensure placement of content in a predictable manner. That is, the reader knows fully well where to find  heading,  menu, menu items, sidebars, footer, main content etc. A site following uniform site-wide experience or a magazine following a uniform writing style ensures that the reader can directly look or find the desired section at a predictable place. However, if we ask tte creator of such articles on how they decide where to place a given section, they may respond that they use article or book/magazine template provided by the site owners or publishers. There are several ways by which uniform reader or creator experience can be ensured and providing templates for slides, documents, articles, blogs etc. is a commonly used approach. The template users know where to place images, sections, footnotes, references etc. Templates usually ensure uniform font styles and sizes for titles, headings of sections and subsections. The advantage is that the readers and content creators need to focus only their work and not be worried about being distracted or managing the style of a document, blogpost or a newspaper article.

Partial Machine Readability

Now, if we turn our attention to the developers. Structured content could mean JSON (or XML some years ago). It need not simply mean the use of JSON or XML. There are several other data formats like YAML or markdown that exist to ensure proper structure to data. Nevertheless, the use of such data formats is indeed the first step. Some of the above data formats also come with the way to guarantee homogeneous representation of the complete data.

Full Machine Readability

Nonetheless, these formats in their classical forms are semi-structured data formats. They are capable to provide homogeneity to the user data. However, machines may not be able to distinguish between the different sections of the data, the purpose and contents of each data snippet. To a certain, extent, some of these data formats ensure human-readability, but they were not fully designed to ensure machine-readability.

As a developer, it's quite usual to start testing simple messages on the screen. But as software becomes stable and more users start using a software, it is equality important to provide interfaces that contain structured content.

Should the application from the very beginning be driven by structured content. I think so. But if you start developing an application with a simple proof of concept, it may be a little difficult to active such an application development.
