Languages have some interesting aspects. Two of which are synonyms and antonyms. Where synonyms can be used to find similar words for a given word, antonyms in contrast gives you a word totally opposite to the given word. And the way by antonym works is interesting. One feels that the languages assume an underlying duality.

Duality may be referred to binary choices, where everything can be limited to yes or no questions. Where if some statement is not affirmative, we may need to find another way to say it in affirmative and antonyms come in picture. "Is this not hot?" can be rephrased as "Is this cold?". Hot and cold, day and night, dawn and dusk, good and bad, long and short, etc. are some words coupled along with their antonyms. One may get an impression that languages are meant to express the extremities. How to represent the states in between. Languages have adjectives like very, much, less, more to specify extremes of the extreme states. Yet, one cannot completely ignore words like noon, which refers to an interval somewhere in the middle of dawn and dusk. Words like warm, lukewarm can be used to say for objects with a temperature between hot and cold. Use of such words are subjective, even though they may seem objective. What is hot for one person may be cold for another.

Even though we have got very precise measuring devices that can give us accurate temperatures or accurate time of the day, duality in languages somehow force us to pigeon-hole objects into two extreme categories. The experience associated to the extreme categories are subjective. Some languages have words for comparison between two objects? Taller, bigger, smaller, shorter, etc. are such comparison words. Though comparison words seem interesting, since they can give a rough idea about which of the two is more closer to the extreme, one needs to wonder the veracity of the extreme condition expressed in the sentence. Interesting, there is no manual which relates a particular temperature or a range of temperature can be referred to as hot or as cold.

Color

Can colors be considered as a counter-example? We do have black and white colors along with gray to specify a color somewhere in between. Our colour perception varies. When it comes to colors other than white, we have got a wide variety of colours. Once again, we have no clue where the boundaries of a color and where it ends. Just ask any person to give the name of a color pointing to a particular point in the gray scale, color scale or a color palette. This will eventually lead us to an interesting debate.

Gender

Another example is how some ancient and even a few recent docments focused on a particular gender, totally ignoring the other genders. Duality in languages assumed for quite a long time that there are only two genders: man and woman.

Growing up, I saw a lot of documents, especially the user manuals assumed the gender of a user and used possessive pronouns like his or pronouns like he. Another approach was the usages like (s)he, his/her, etc. to ensure inclusive writing. Though it was the first major step, it still remains incomplete. Some authors in the past unable to find an inclusive word, introduced a new word to signify all persons in the beginning of a text. Some chose a particular pronoun and clarified that its usage must be seen as inclusive. Fortunately, recent years have seen a growing awareness towards inclusive writing and the use of gender-neutral words. For example, gender-neutral pronouns like singular they can be seen in many new texts.

Conclusion

We do have precise scientific terms for human species: homo sapiens sapiens. We also have precise measuring devices and precise ways to represent different measurements. Yet their usage is quite rare outside the scientific and technical circles. It is time to encourage the use of precise words and also ensure inclusive writing.
